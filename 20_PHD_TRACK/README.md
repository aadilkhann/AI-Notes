## 1. Should You Do a PhD?

### 1.1 What is a PhD?

**Definition**: Doctor of Philosophy - Research degree focused on advancing human knowledge.

**Duration**: 4-7 years (typical: 5-6 in US, 3-4 in Europe)

**Goal**: Make original contributions to your field.

### 1.2 Good Reasons to Do a PhD

✅ **Passion for research**: Love discovering new things  
✅ **Specific research question**: Deep problem you want to solve  
✅ **Academic career**: Professor/research scientist path  
✅ **Intellectual freedom**: Explore ideas deeply  
✅ **Elite research labs**: Access to cutting-edge resources  
✅ **Long-term learning**: Develop expertise over years

### 1.3 Bad Reasons

❌ **Prestige**: "Dr." title isn't worth 5 years  
❌ **Avoiding job market**: Industry pays better  
❌ **Following peers**: Very personal decision  
❌ **Better salary**: Entry-level PhD ≈ mid-level engineer  
❌ **Uncertain about career**: Try industry first

### 1.4 PhD vs Industry

| Aspect | PhD | Industry |
|--------|-----|----------|
| **Duration** | 5-6 years | Start immediately |
| **Income** | $30-40k/year | $150-400k/year (ML roles) |
| **Freedom** | High (research direction) | Medium (product constraints) |
| **Impact** | Long-term, foundational | Short-term, applied |
| **Prestige** | Academic recognition | Product/company impact |
| **Work-life balance** | Often poor | Usually better |
| **Job flexibility** | Niche roles | Broad opportunities |

**Opportunity cost**: PhD (5 years) vs Industry (5 years)
- **PhD**: $200k stipend + degree + publications
- **Industry**: $1.5-2M earnings + experience + investment returns

### 1.5 Hybrid Paths

**Research Engineer** at industry labs:
- Google Research, Meta AI, Microsoft Research, OpenAI, Anthropic
- Do research without PhD constraints
- Better pay, more resources

**Part-time PhD**:
- Work in industry while pursuing PhD
- Takes 6-8 years
- Best of both worlds

**Masters → Industry → PhD**:
- Gain practical experience first
- Return with clarity on research interests
- More mature researcher

### 2.3 Application Components

**1. Statement of Purpose (SoP)**

**Structure** (2 pages):
- **Introduction** (¶1): Research interests, why PhD
- **Research experience** (¶2-4): Projects, contributions, learnings
- **Future research** (¶5): What you want to work on, why this school
- **Fit** (¶6): Why you + specific advisor = great match
- **Conclusion** (¶7): Reiterate passion, readiness

**Tips**:
- Be specific (not "I'm interested in AI" but "I want to improve LLM reasoning via neuro-symbolic methods")
- Show technical depth
- Name 2-3 faculty you'd work with
- Demonstrate fit with program strengths

**2. Letters of Recommendation (3-4)**

**Best recommenders**:
- Research advisor who supervised your work
- Professor you did independent study with
- Industry researcher you collaborated with

**What makes a strong letter**:
- Specific examples of your contributions
- Comparison to other students
- Potential for research impact
- Work ethic and collaboration

**How to request**:
- Ask early (2-3 months before deadline)
- Provide: CV, SoP draft, project descriptions
- Remind politely as deadline approaches

**3. Research Statement** (optional, some programs)

**Structure** (3-5 pages):
- **Past research**: Technical depth
- **Future directions**: Open problems
- **Why this matters**: Impact

**4. CV**

**Include**:
- Education
- Research experience
- Publications (if any)
- Technical skills
- Honors & awards
- Teaching experience

**5. Transcripts**

**What they look for**:
- Strong grades in ML/math courses
- Upward trajectory
- Relevant coursework

**6. GRE** (Many programs now waive)

**If required**:
- Quant: Aim for 165+ (170 ideal)
- Verbal: 155+ (160 ideal)
- Writing: 4.0+

**When**: 2-3 months before application deadline

### 2.5 PhD Interviews

**Format**: 30-60 min video call

**Questions to expect**:
- Walk me through your research.
- What did you contribute specifically?
- What are the limitations of your work?
- What do you want to work on in PhD?
- Why our group?
- How do you handle failure?

**Questions to ask**:
- What are current research directions in your group?
- How many students do you typically advise?
- What's the lab culture like?
- Funding situation?
- Graduation timeline expectations?

**Red flags**:
- Advisor has >10 students
- Students take 7+ years
- High attrition rate
- advisor micromanages / is absent

## 3. Research Methodology

### 3.1 Formulating Research Questions

**Bad**: "Make AI better"  
**Good**: "Improve compositional generalization in transformers via modular architectures"

**Characteristics of good research questions**:
- **Specific**: Clear problem definition
- **Novel**: Not already solved
- **Impactful**: Matters to community
- **Feasible**: Can be done in reasonable time with available resources

**Framework: PICOT**
- **P**roblem: What's broken?
- **I**ntervention: Your proposed solution
- **C**omparison: Baseline/alternatives
- **O**utcome: How to measure success
- **T**imeframe: When can you evaluate?

### 3.2 Literature Review

**Process**:
1. **Seed papers**: Start with 5-10 seminal papers
2. **Backward search**: Check their references
3. **Forward search**: Papers that cite them (Google Scholar)
4. **Survey papers**: Comprehensive overviews
5. **Recent work**: Last 6-12 months on arXiv

**Tools**:
- **Semantic Scholar**: AI-powered search
- **Connected Papers**: Visual citation graphs
- **ArXiv Sanity**: Browse ML papers
- **Papers with Code**: Find implementations

**Organization**:
- **Zotero**: Reference manager
- **Notion/Obsidian**: Note-taking linked to papers
- **Tags**: Organize by topic, method, dataset

### 3.3 Experimental Design

**Scientific method**:
1. **Hypothesis**: "Sparse attention improves long-context modeling"
2. **Experiment**: Compare sparse vs dense attention on long sequences
3. **Measure**: Perplexity, downstream task performance
4. **Analyze**: Statistical significance
5. **Conclude**: Accept/reject hypothesis
6. **Iterate**: Refine based on results

**Controls**:
- **Ablations**: Remove components to show necessity
- **Baselines**: Compare to state-of-the-art
- **Hyperparameters**: Fair comparison (same tuning budget)

**Statistical rigor**:
- Multiple random seeds (report mean ± std)
- Significance tests (t-test, Wilcoxon)
- Confidence intervals

### 3.4 Reproducibility

**Make your research reproducible**:
- **Code**: Open-source on GitHub
- **Data**: Share or document access
- **Hyperparameters**: Log everything
- **Random seeds**: Fix for determinism
- **Environment**: Docker container
- **Dependencies**: requirements.txt, poetry.lock

**Gold standard**:
```
README with:
- Setup instructions
- Data download/preprocessing scripts
- Training commands with exact flags
- Evaluation scripts
- Expected results
- Hardware requirements (# GPUs, hours)
```

## 4. Reading Research Papers at PhD Level

### 4.1 Critical Reading

**Questions to ask**:
- **Motivation**: Is the problem well-motivated? Is it important?
- **Novelty**: What's new? How different from prior work?
- **Technical soundness**: Are there flaws in method/theory?
- **Experiments**: Are they convincing? What's missing?
- **Limitations**: What are the assumptions? When does it fail?
- **Impact**: Will this paper matter in 5 years?

### 4.2 Beyond Surface Level

**Reproduce key experiments**:
- Implement algorithm from paper
- Run on toy dataset
- Verify results match

**Extend the work**:
- Try different dataset
- Change assumptions
- Combine with other methods

**Build on it**:
- Identify limitations
- Propose improvements
- Conduct follow-up experiment

### 4.3 Paper Reading Groups

**Format**:
- Weekly meetings (1-2 hours)
- Each week: 1 person presents paper
- Presenter: 20 min summary + discussion questions
- Group: Discussion, critique, brainstorm extensions

**Benefits**:
- Accountability (must read deeply)
- Diverse perspectives
- Practice presenting
- Build research community

## 5. Conducting Original Research

### 5.1 Research Workflow

**Phase 1: Ideation (2-4 weeks)**
- Read extensively
- Replicate prior work
- Identify gaps
- Brainstorm solutions

**Phase 2: Prototyping (4-8 weeks)**
- Implement baseline
- Try simplest version of idea
- Run preliminary experiments
- Quick feedback loop

**Phase 3: Experimentation (8-16 weeks)**
- Systematic evaluation
- Ablation studies
- Compare to SOTA
- Try multiple datasets

**Phase 4: Writing (4-8 weeks)**
- Draft paper
- Make figures
- Get feedback
- Revise

**Phase 5: Submission & Review (3-6 months)**
- Submit to conference
- Address reviewer feedback
- Resubmit if rejected

### 5.2 Common Pitfalls

**Analysis paralysis**: Reading forever, never implementing  
→ **Solution**: Timebox reading to 2 weeks, then code

**Chasing SOTA**: Incrementally improving benchmarks without insight  
→ **Solution**: Focus on understanding, not just numbers

**Ignoring baselines**: Claiming improvement without fair comparison  
→ **Solution**: Reimplement baselines with same setup

**Premature scaling**: Trying large-scale experiments before proof of concept  
→ **Solution**: Start small, scale once idea works

**Confirmation bias**: Only showing results that support hypothesis  
→ **Solution**: Report negative results, be honest about limitations

### 5.3 When to Pivot

**Signals idea isn't working**:
- No improvement after 10+ variations
- Fundamentally flawed assumption
- Better alternative discovered
- Problem becomes less interesting

**How to pivot**:
- Don't abandon immediately (explore thoroughly)
- Discuss with advisor
- Salvage parts that worked
- Document learnings

**Sunk cost fallacy**: Don't continue bad project just because you've invested time.

## 6. Writing Research Papers

### 6.1 Paper Structure

**1. Abstract** (200-250 words)
- Problem (2-3 sentences)
- Solution (2-3 sentences)
- Key results  (2-3 sentences)
- Impact (1 sentence)

**2. Introduction**
- Motivate problem (1-2 pages)
- Limitations of existing work
- Your contribution (bullet points)
- Paper organization

**3. Related Work**
- Survey relevant literature
- Group by theme
- Contrast with your approach
- Can be before or after methods

**4. Method**
- Technical details
- Mathematical notation
- Algorithms (pseudocode)
- Architecture diagrams

**5. Experiments**
- Datasets
- Baselines
- Implementation details
- Results (tables, figures)
- Ablation studies
- Analysis

**6. Conclusion**
- Summarize contributions
- Limitations
- Future work

**7. Appendix** (if needed)
- Proofs
- Additional experiments
- Hyperparameters
- Negative results

### 6.2 Writing Tips

**Clarity over cleverness**:
- Simple sentences
- Avoid jargon where possible
- Define terminology

**Show, don't tell**:
- Figures > paragraphs
- Examples over abstract descriptions
- Code snippets for clarity

**Mathematical rigor**:
- Define all notation
- State assumptions explicitly
- Include proofs (or defer to appendix)

**Honesty**:
- Report limitations
- Acknowledge when baseline is strong
- Show negative results in appendix

**Revision**:
- First draft always terrible
- Revise 5-10 times
- Get feedback from advisor + peers

### 6.3 LaTeX Best Practices

**Document structure**:
```latex
\documentclass{article}
\usepackage{neurips_2024} % Conference style

\usepackage{amsmath, amssymb}
\usepackage{graphicx}
\usepackage{algorithm, algorithmic}
\usepackage{hyperref}

\title{Your Paper Title}
\author{You, Advisor, Collaborators}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
...
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
...

\bibliographystyle{plain}
\bibliography{references}

\end{document}
```

**Math formatting**:
```latex
% Equations
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p(x)} [-\log q_\theta(x)]
\end{equation}

% Align multiple equations
\begin{align}
\mu &= \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} x_i \\
\sigma^2 &= \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (x_i - \mu)^2
\end{align}
```

**Figures**:
```latex
\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figures/architecture.pdf}
\caption{Model architecture. Attention mechanism (blue) processes input sequence (left) and generates output (right).}
\label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

% Reference in text:
See Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} for details.
```

**Tables**:
```latex
\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Results on benchmark datasets. * indicates our method.}
\label{tab:results}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Method & Accuracy & F1 \\
\midrule
Baseline & 85.2 & 83.1 \\
Prior SOTA & 87.5 & 85.3 \\
Ours* & \textbf{89.1} & \textbf{87.2} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}
```

### 6.4 Collaboration & Authorship

**Author order** (ML conventions):
- **First author**: Primary contributor
- **Last author**: Senior advisor (PhD advisor)
- **Middle authors**: Collaborators, ordered by contribution

**Authorship criteria**:
- Substantial intellectual contribution
- Writing/editing sections
- Running key experiments
- Regular meetings and feedback

**Gift authorship**: ❌ Don't add people who didn't contribute

**Communication**:
- Discuss authorship early
- Clarify roles and expectations
- Use shared Overleaf for writing

## 7. PhD Survival Guide

### 7.1 Advisor Relationship

**Choosing advisor** (most important decision):
- **Research fit**: Aligned interests
- **Advising style**: Hands-on vs hands-off
- **Funding**: Stable grants
- **Lab culture**: Collaborative, supportive
- **Track record**: Students graduate, get good jobs

**Red flags**:
- No funding plan
- Advisor never in office
- Students unhappy/leaving
- Micromanages or neglects

**Working relationship**:
- **Weekly meetings**: Report progress, get feedback
- **Set clear goals**: What to accomplish by when
- **Communicate early**: If stuck, don't wait to say so
- **Be proactive**: Come with ideas, not just problems

### 7.2 Time Management

**PhD is marathon, not sprint**:
- Work 40-50 hours/week (sustainable)
- Take weekends off
- Vacation: 2-3 weeks/year

**Daily routine** (example):
```
9:00 - 10:00   Read papers, respond to emails
10:00 - 12:30  Deep work (coding, experiments)
12:30 - 1:30   Lunch, walk
1:30 - 4:00    Deep work
4:00 - 4:30    Coffee, socialize
4:30 - 6:00    Meetings, writing, admin
6:00 - 7:00    Exercise, dinner
7:00 - 10:00   Personal time
```

**Avoid**:
- ❌ All-nighters (except rare deadlines)
- ❌ Working 7 days/week
- ❌ Guilt about taking breaks

### 7.3 Mental Health

**PhD is hard**:
- Isolation (deep focus)
- Imposter syndrome (everyone feels this)
- Slow progress (research is uncertain)
- Comparison to peers
- Uncertainty about future

**Coping strategies**:
- **Community**: Build friendships with cohort
- **Therapy**: Most universities offer free counseling
- **Exercise**: 30 min/day, huge mood boost
- **Hobbies**: Non-academic interests
- **Boundaries**: Work-life separation
- **Celebrate small wins**: Paper accepted, experiment works

**Red flags** (seek help):
- Constant anxiety/depression
- Sleep problems (insomnia, oversleeping)
- Loss of interest in research
- Feeling trapped

**Resources**:
- University counseling center
- PhD support groups
- National hotlines

### 7.4 Milestones

**Year 1**:
- Coursework (if required)
- Rotate through labs (some programs)
- TAship (teaching assistant)
- Explore research directions

**Year 2**:
- Finalize advisor
- First paper submission
- Qualifier/comprehensive exam (some programs)

**Year 3**:
- Deep into research
- 1-2 publications
- Conference presentations

**Year 4**:
- Thesis proposal
- More publications
- Internships (optional)

**Year 5-6**:
- Finish thesis
- Job market (faculty or industry)
- Dissertation defense
- Graduate!

### 7.5 Courses vs Research

**Coursework** (Year 1-2):
- Build foundations
- Explore breadth
- Don't over-optimize grades (B+ is fine)

**After coursework**:
- 100% research
- Take courses only if directly relevant

**Audit seminars**:
- Weekly ML/AI seminars
- Reading groups
- Workshops

## 9. Publishing & Conferences

### 9.1 Conference Tier List

**Tier 1** (Top-tier, hardest):
- **NeurIPS**: Largest ML conference (15-20% acceptance)
- **ICML**: Core ML theory/methods (20-25%)
- **ICLR**: Representation learning, increasingly broad (25-30%)

**Tier 2** (Strong):
- **CVPR**: Computer vision (25%)
- **EMNLP/ACL**: NLP (20-25%)
- **AAAI**: Broad AI (20%)
- **KDD**: Data mining, applied ML (15%)

**Tier 3** (Good):
- **WACV**: Vision (30%)
- **NAACL**: NLP, North America (25%)
- **IJCAI**: Broad AI (25%)

**Workshops**:
- Co-located with main conferences
- Faster review, less prestigious
- Good for early-stage work

### 9.2 Submission Process

**1. Prepare submission**:
- Follow formatting guidelines exactly
- Check page limits (8-10 pages typical)
- Anonymize for double-blind review

**2. Submit via CMT/OpenReview**:
- Upload PDF
- Fill out metadata
- Suggest reviewers (optional)
- Conflicts of interest

**3. Review period (2-3 months)**:
- 3-4 reviewers read paper
- Assign scores (1-10 scale)
- Write reviews (strengths, weaknesses, questions)

**4. Rebuttal (1 week)**:
- Respond to reviewer concerns
- Clarify misunderstandings
- Promise additional experiments
- Be professional, not defensive

**5. Decision**:
- **Accept**: 🎉 Congratulations!
- **Reject**: Most common, revise and resubmit elsewhere
- **Borderline**: Rebuttal can make difference

**6. Camera-ready** (if accepted):
- Incorporate reviewer feedback
- Final PDF due 2-4 weeks after acceptance

**7. Presentation**:
- Oral (15-20 min talk) or Poster (3-hour session)
- Prepare slides/poster

### 9.3 Dealing with Rejection

**Reality**: Most papers get rejected (50-80% rejection rate).

**Mindset**:
- Not personal
- Reviewers may be wrong
- Part of the process

**What to do**:
1. Read reviews carefully (often have valid points)
2. Discuss with advisor
3. Decide: Revise and resubmit, or move on
4. Improve paper based on feedback
5. Submit to next conference

**Example**: Paper rejected from NeurIPS → improve → accept to ICML.

### 9.4 Conference Attendance

**Benefits of attending**:
- Present your work
- Network with researchers
- Learn about latest work
- Get internship/job opportunities

**How to maximize**:
- **Before**: Read accepted papers, note talks/posters of interest
- **During**: 
  - Attend keynotes
  - Ask questions at talks
  - Visit posters (yours and others)
  - Socialize at receptions
  - Exchange contact info
- **After**: Follow up with connections

**Funding**: Department usually covers registration + travel

## 10. Post-PhD Career Paths

### 10.1 Academia

**Tenure-track professor**:
- **Pros**: Research freedom, train students, job security (after tenure)
- **Cons**: Grant writing, teaching load, slow salary growth
- **Timeline**: Postdoc (2-3 years) → Assistant Prof (6 years) → Tenure

**Requirements**:
- Strong publication record (10-20+ papers)
- Prestigious PhD
- Postdoc at top institution
- Research vision

**Salary** (US, 2026):
- Assistant Professor: $90-150k
- Associate Professor: $120-180k
- Full Professor: $150-250k

### 10.2 Industry Research

**Research Scientist** (Google, Meta, Microsoft, OpenAI, Anthropic):
- **Pros**: High salary ($200-500k), resources, impact at scale
- **Cons**: Product constraints, less freedom than academia
- **Path**: PhD → Research Scientist → Senior → Principal

**Research Engineer**:
- Implement research ideas
- Bridge research and product
- Strong engineering + ML knowledge

**Applied Scientist**:
- Apply ML to products
- More applied than research scientist

### 10.3 Startups

**Founding**:
- Turn PhD work into company
- High risk, high reward
- Examples: Anthropic (Dario Amodei), Adept (David Luan)

**Early employee**:
- Join AI startup as founding ML engineer
- Equity upside, faster impact
- Less structured than big tech

### 10.4 Hybrid Roles

**Consulting**:
- Part-time advising for companies
- Maintain academic position

**Adjunct Professor**:
- Teach while working in industry
- Best of both worlds

**Research Professor**:
- No teaching, pure research
- At some universities

### 10.5 Salary Comparison (2026, US)

| Path | Early Career | Mid Career | Late Career |
|------|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| **Tenure-track** | $90-120k | $120-180k | $150-250k |
| **Research Scientist** | $200-300k | $300-500k | $500k-1M+ |
| **Startup (founder)** | $0-100k | Variable | $0 or $$$M |
| **Industry ML** | $200-350k | $350-600k | $600k-1.5M |

**Non-monetary factors matter**:
- Research freedom
- Mission/values
- Work-life balance
- Location
- Team

- **Progress over perfection**: Incremental progress beats analysis paralysis
- **Community over competition**: Collaborate, share, help others
- **Persistence over brilliance**: Grit matters more than genius
- **Balance over burnout**: Take care of yourself

The AI field is young and rapidly evolving. There's room for many brilliant minds to make their mark. 

**Go do great research. The world needs it.** 🚀

---

## Checklist: PhD Readiness

**Research experience**:
- [ ] 1-2 years of research (undergrad/masters/industry)
- [ ] At least 1 paper (published, submitted, or preprint)
- [ ] Implemented papers from scratch
- [ ] Contributed to open-source ML projects

**Technical skills**:
- [ ] Strong coding (Python, PyTorch/TensorFlow)
- [ ] Math foundations (linear algebra, probability, optimization)
- [ ] Read 50+ research papers critically
- [ ] Reproduced results from 3+ papers

**Soft skills**:
- [ ] Written technical reports
- [ ] Presented research (orally or poster)
- [ ] Collaborated on team projects
- [ ] Handled deadlines and setbacks

**Logistics**:
- [ ] Strong GPA (3.5+ in relevant courses)
- [ ] 3 recommendation letters lined up
- [ ] Identified 10-15 target programs
- [ ] Researched potential advisors
- [ ] Drafted Statement of Purpose

**Mindset**:
- [ ] Genuinely excited about research
- [ ] Comfortable with uncertainty
- [ ] Willing to commit 5-6 years
- [ ] Financially prepared (low salary)
- [ ] Mentally prepared (high stress)

**If you checked most boxes, you're ready to apply!**

If not, consider:
- Research master's program
- Industry research role
- More time in current role building experience

**There's no one path. Find what works for you.** 🎓
